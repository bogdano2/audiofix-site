Headphones not detected on Windows 11?

If Windows 11 doesn't detect your headphones, the most common cause is the device being disabled in Sound Settings, or plugged into the wrong jack. Open Sound Settings (Win+plusR, type ms-settings:sound, Enter), scroll to Advanced, and click More sound settings to see if your headphones are listed but disabled.

Check if the device is disabled

Windows 11 hides disabled audio devices from the default view. Your headphones might be right there in the system — they're just invisible. This is the number-one reason headphones appear to be "not detected" when they're actually recognized by Windows but switched off.

This is the #1 cause of "headphones not detected." A disabled device looks exactly like a missing device from the user's perspective, but the fix takes about ten seconds.

Here's how to reveal hidden devices and re-enable your headphones:

  1. Press Win+plusR, type ms-settings:sound, press Enter.
  2. Scroll down to the Advanced section and click More sound settings. This opens the classic Sound dialog.
  3. In the Playback tab, right-click any empty space in the device list.
  4. Check both Show Disabled Devices and Show Disconnected Devices.
  5. If your headphones now appear greyed out, right-click them and select Enable.
  6. Right-click the headphones again and select Set as Default Device.

Play some audio. If you hear it, you're done. The headphones were there all along — Windows just wasn't showing them to you.

Why do devices get disabled? You (or someone else) may have disabled it previously to prevent audio switching problems. Windows Update can also reset device states, and some driver installers disable devices during setup and don't re-enable them.

Wired headphones: check the physical connection

If your headphones didn't appear even after showing disabled devices, the issue may be physical. Not all audio jacks are created equal, and plugging into the wrong one is more common than you'd think.

Front panel vs. rear panel

Desktop PCs have audio jacks in two places: the front panel (on the case) and the rear panel (on the motherboard). The rear jacks connect directly to the audio chipset and almost always work. The front panel jacks, however, depend on a thin ribbon cable connecting the case to a header on the motherboard.

Front panel not working? If your headphones work in the rear jack but not the front, the internal front-panel audio header cable may not be connected to the motherboard. This is common in custom-built PCs or after motherboard replacements. Opening the case and checking the header labeled HD_AUDIO or AAFP on the motherboard will confirm.

Combo jacks: TRRS vs. TRS

Many headsets (especially phone-style ones with an inline microphone) use a TRRS plug — that's a 3.5mm jack with four metal segments (tip, ring, ring, sleeve). Most desktop PCs have separate TRS jacks (three segments) for headphones and microphone.

If you plug a TRRS headset into a TRS-only jack, three things can happen: it might work fine, it might only play audio from one ear, or Windows might not detect it at all. The fix is a TRRS-to-dual-TRS splitter cable, which separates the headphone and microphone signals into the two jacks your PC expects.

Try a different port

Before troubleshooting software, rule out the jack itself. Plug your headphones into the rear green jack (the standard line-out on most motherboards). If they work there but not in the front, the problem is physical, not software.

USB headphones and headsets

USB headphones have their own built-in sound card (a DAC and amplifier inside the USB plug or inline control box). Windows treats them as a completely separate audio device from your motherboard audio. That means they need their own driver, and if that driver doesn't load correctly, the headphones won't appear in Sound Settings at all.

  1. Try a different USB port. USB 2.0 ports (usually black) are more compatible than USB 3.0 (blue) for older headsets. Avoid USB hubs — plug directly into the PC.
  2. Check Device Manager. Press Win+plusR, type devmgmt.msc, press Enter. Look under Sound, video and game controllers and Other devices for anything with a yellow triangle or labeled Unknown device.
  3. Unplug, wait 10 seconds, and replug. This forces Windows to re-enumerate the USB device. Watch Device Manager while you plug it in — you should see a new entry appear under Sound controllers.
USB-C headphones on older PCs: If your headphones use USB-C and your PC only has USB-A ports, make sure you're using a data-capable adapter, not a charge-only cable. Charge-only adapters don't carry the data lines that audio requires.

Bluetooth headphones

Bluetooth headphones have their own set of detection issues that go beyond what wired headphones encounter. If your Bluetooth headphones are paired but not showing up as an audio device, run through this quick checklist:

  • Are they in pairing mode? Most Bluetooth headphones need to be in pairing mode (usually by holding the power button for 5+ seconds) to be discovered by a new PC.
  • Are they connected to another device? Many Bluetooth headphones can only connect to one device at a time. Disconnect them from your phone or tablet first.
  • Is Bluetooth enabled on the PC? Press Win+plusR, type ms-settings:bluetooth, press Enter. Make sure the Bluetooth toggle is on.
  • Remove and re-pair. In Bluetooth settings, find the headphones, click the three dots, and select Remove device. Then pair them again from scratch.

Bluetooth audio problems have enough nuance to fill their own page — dual profiles (stereo vs. hands-free), codec issues, and driver quirks. For the complete walkthrough, see Bluetooth audio not working.

Update or reinstall audio drivers

If your headphones still aren't showing up, the audio driver may be corrupted, outdated, or simply missing. The driver is what tells Windows how to communicate with the audio hardware — without it, Windows doesn't know the jack exists.

  1. Press Win+plusR, type devmgmt.msc, press Enter to open Device Manager.
  2. Expand Sound, video and game controllers.
  3. Right-click your audio device (commonly Realtek(R) Audio or High Definition Audio Device) and select Update driver.
  4. Choose Search automatically for drivers. Windows will check for a newer version.
  5. If Windows says the best driver is already installed, try the manufacturer's website instead. For Realtek, download the latest driver from realtek.com/Downloads or your motherboard manufacturer's support page.
Windows Update sometimes installs the wrong driver. Microsoft's driver catalog includes generic audio drivers that can replace a working Realtek driver with a less capable one. If your headphones stopped being detected after a Windows Update, this may be why. Download the driver directly from your motherboard manufacturer's website to get the correct version.

If updating doesn't help, try a clean reinstall: right-click the device in Device Manager, select Uninstall device, check the box to Attempt to remove the driver for this device, and confirm. Then click Action → Scan for hardware changes in the Device Manager menu bar. Windows will reinstall the driver from scratch.

Still not detected? Check Device Manager

If none of the above steps worked, Device Manager can tell you whether Windows recognizes the hardware at all — even if it can't use it yet.

  1. Press Win+plusR, type devmgmt.msc, press Enter.
  2. Expand Sound, video and game controllers. Look for any entry with a yellow triangle icon. That means Windows sees the hardware but can't communicate with it properly.
  3. Also check Other devices at the bottom. An "Unknown device" entry that appeared when you plugged in your headphones means the driver is completely missing.
  4. Click View → Show hidden devices in the menu bar. This reveals devices that were once installed but are now disconnected or have ghost entries. Your headphones may appear here.

For any device with a yellow triangle or "Unknown device" label:

  1. Right-click the device and select Uninstall device.
  2. Unplug the headphones (if USB or Bluetooth, disconnect them).
  3. Click Action → Scan for hardware changes.
  4. Plug the headphones back in. Windows will attempt to detect them fresh and install the appropriate driver.
No audio devices at all in Device Manager? If the entire "Sound, video and game controllers" category is missing, your audio chipset driver isn't installed. Visit your motherboard or laptop manufacturer's support page, download the audio driver for your model, and install it manually.
