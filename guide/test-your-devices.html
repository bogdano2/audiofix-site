Test your devices

You found your devices and defaults in the last step. Now let's prove which device is actually your speakers and which is actually your microphone.

Testing output: play sound, switch devices, listen

The simplest test. Play audio from any source (YouTube, Spotify, a video file), then switch between output devices one at a time. Listen for which one produces sound from your actual speakers or headphones.

Steps

  1. Start playing any audio
  2. Open Sound Settings — Win + R → type ms-settings:sound → press Enter
  3. Click each output device in the dropdown
  4. Listen — which one plays through your intended speakers?
Keep the audio playing the whole time. When you switch devices, the sound should jump to the new device instantly.

No sound from any device?

If you can't hear audio from any output device, check these first:

  • Is the volume slider above zero in Sound Settings?
  • Is the speaker icon showing a mute symbol? Click to unmute.
  • Are your speakers/headphones physically powered on?
  • For Bluetooth: is the device actually connected (not just paired)? Check Settings → Bluetooth & devices.
  • Try a different audio source (browser vs. media player) — some apps override the OS default.
If nothing works from any device, the problem may be a driver issue. Try: Settings → Windows Update → Check for updates.

Testing input: speak, watch the level meter

Switch to each input device, speak into your microphone, and watch the input level bar in Sound Settings. The bar moves when the active device picks up your voice.

Steps

  1. In Sound Settings, scroll to the Input section
  2. Select the first microphone from the dropdown
  3. Speak at normal volume
  4. Watch the level indicator — does the bar move?
  5. If not, try the next device

Use the built-in mic test for confirmation

Windows has a built-in microphone test that gives you a definitive answer. No guessing, no squinting at a level bar.

Steps

  1. Click on the input device name to open its properties
  2. Scroll to "Test your microphone"
  3. Click "Start test" → speak for 3–5 seconds → click "Stop test"
  4. Any result above 0% confirms this mic hears you
This is the most reliable test. The level meter can be subtle — the percentage doesn't lie.

Shortcut: Win + R → ms-settings:sound-defaultinputproperties opens the input device properties directly.

Mic test shows 0% on every device?

If no input device registers your voice, the issue is likely hardware or connection, not software. Check these:

  • Is the mic muted? Check for a physical mute button on the mic or headset.
  • Wired mic: is it in the right jack? Usually pink for mic, green for speakers.
  • USB mic: try a different USB port.
  • Bluetooth: confirm it's connected, not just paired. Some Bluetooth headsets have separate audio profiles — make sure "Hands-Free" or "Headset" profile is active, not just "Stereo."
  • Is the input volume/sensitivity slider above zero?

Write down what works

Now you know which output device and which input device are real. Write down their exact names from the Sound Settings dropdown.

You'll need these names in the next step when you disable everything else.

The device names in Settings don't always match what you'd expect. "Speakers (Realtek(R) Audio)" could be your laptop speakers OR a docking station's audio. The test you just did is the only reliable way to know.
