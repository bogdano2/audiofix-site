Connect Bluetooth headphones or speaker to Windows 11

Windows macOS coming soon Linux coming soon

To connect Bluetooth headphones, earbuds, or a speaker to Windows 11, put your device in pairing mode, open Bluetooth Settings (Win+plusR, type ms-settings:bluetooth, Enter), click Add device, select Bluetooth, and click your device when it appears.

Step 1: Put your device in pairing mode

This is the step most people skip — and it's the reason Windows can't find their device. Bluetooth pairing only works when the device is actively broadcasting that it's available. If your headphones are just turned on but not in pairing mode, they'll try to reconnect to whatever they were last paired with (usually your phone) and Windows will never see them.

Every Bluetooth device has a specific way to enter pairing mode. For most headphones and speakers, it involves holding down a button for several seconds until an LED starts flashing a specific color (usually blue or white). The exact method depends on the manufacturer.

Example: Apple AirPods Pro

  1. Open the AirPods charging case lid (keep the AirPods inside the case).
  2. Press and hold the setup button on the back of the case — it's the small, round button below the hinge.
  3. Wait until the status light between the AirPods flashes white. This takes about 3–5 seconds.
  4. The AirPods are now discoverable by Windows. Don't close the case lid until pairing is complete.
Every device is different. If you don't know how to enter pairing mode, search for "[your device name] pairing mode" — the manufacturer's support page will have the exact steps.
If your device was previously paired with a phone or tablet (especially Apple devices), it may auto-connect there instead. Turn off Bluetooth on the other device first, or put your headphones in pairing mode again.

Step 2: Pair in Windows Settings

With your device in pairing mode, it's time to tell Windows to look for it. This part is straightforward:

  1. Press Win+plusR, type ms-settings:bluetooth, press Enter.
  2. Make sure Bluetooth is toggled On at the top of the page.
  3. Click Add device.
  4. Select Bluetooth (the first option in the list — the other options are for different wireless protocols that audio devices don't use).
  5. Wait for your device to appear in the list. This may take a few seconds — don't click around or navigate away, because leaving this screen cancels the Bluetooth discovery scan and you'll have to start over.
  6. Click your device name to pair.
  7. You should see a "Connected" or "Paired" confirmation.
Once paired, the device will automatically reconnect whenever it's turned on and in range — no need to repeat this process.

It paired but there's no sound

Bluetooth headsets show up as two separate devices in Windows — one for high-quality stereo audio (labeled "Headphones") and one for the hands-free mono profile (labeled "Headset" or "Hands-Free AG Audio"). If Windows set the wrong one as default, you'll hear no sound or terrible quality.

This is the most common post-pairing issue, and it has a straightforward fix. Open Sound Settings (Win+plusR, type ms-settings:sound, Enter) and make sure the stereo "Headphones" profile is selected as your output device — not the "Headset" one.

It won't pair or doesn't appear

If your device doesn't show up in the "Add a device" window, work through this checklist before trying anything more drastic:

  • Is your device in pairing mode? The light should be flashing, not solid. A solid light usually means the device is on but not discoverable.
  • Is Bluetooth turned on in Windows? Open ms-settings:bluetooth and check the toggle at the top.
  • Is the device already connected to another phone or tablet? Most Bluetooth audio devices can only maintain one active connection. Disconnect from the other device first, or turn off Bluetooth on it.
  • Is the device within range? Stay within 1–2 meters during the initial pairing. You can move further away after pairing is complete.
  • Have you tried removing and re-pairing? If the device was previously paired, it may have stale pairing data. In Bluetooth settings, click the device → Remove device, then put the device in pairing mode and add it again.
  • Is the device charged? Low battery can prevent pairing entirely. Charge it to at least 20% and try again.
If nothing works, try restarting the Bluetooth service: press Win+plusR, type services.msc, find "Bluetooth Support Service", right-click → Restart. Then try pairing again.

Your device is connected — but you're not done yet

Pairing is only half the job. If you stop here, your audio will work today — but it will break itself the next time conditions change.

Bluetooth devices disconnect and reconnect constantly — when the battery dies, when you walk out of range, when you switch to your phone for a call. Every time a Bluetooth device reconnects, Windows re-evaluates your audio defaults using a "most recently activated" heuristic. If you don't set this device as your preferred default and disable the devices you don't use, Windows may silently route your audio somewhere else the next time it reconnects.

Think of it like assigned seating vs. first-come-first-served. Right now, your Bluetooth device grabbed a seat because it showed up. But if another device shows up next time (your monitor's HDMI audio, Stereo Mix, a phantom device you didn't know existed), Windows might give the seat away. You need to reserve the seat.

The fix takes 5 minutes and only needs to be done once:

  1. Find your devices — confirm the Bluetooth device appears in Sound Settings and check what else is listed.
  2. Test it — play audio, verify it comes through the right device.
  3. Lock it down — set it as default for all audio roles and disable unused devices so Windows can't silently switch.
