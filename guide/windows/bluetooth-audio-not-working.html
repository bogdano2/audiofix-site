Bluetooth audio not working on Windows 11?

Windows macOS Linux

If your Bluetooth headphones are connected but there's no sound, Windows is probably routing audio to the wrong device. Bluetooth headsets show up as two separate devices in Windows: one for high-quality stereo audio and one for the hands-free (mono) profile. Open Sound Settings (Win+plusR, type ms-settings:sound, Enter) and check which output device is selected.

The Bluetooth dual-device problem

When you pair a Bluetooth headset with Windows, the operating system doesn't create one audio device — it creates two. This is because Bluetooth audio uses different profiles for different purposes, and Windows exposes each profile as a separate audio endpoint.

Think of it like a single phone that has two phone numbers: one for personal calls and one for work. Same physical device, but the system treats them as entirely separate lines.

Here's what you'll see in Sound Settings after pairing a headset called "Sony WH-1000XM5":

Device name in Windows Bluetooth profile Audio quality Microphone Use for
Headphones (Sony WH-1000XM5) A2DP (Advanced Audio Distribution) Stereo, 44.1–96 kHz, high quality No Music, videos, games
Headset (Sony WH-1000XM5 Hands-Free AG Audio) HFP (Hands-Free Profile) Mono, 8–16 kHz, telephone quality Yes Voice calls only
If Hands-Free is your default output device, audio will sound robotic and low quality. This is the single most common cause of "bad Bluetooth audio" on Windows. The Hands-Free profile is designed for voice calls — it's mono and limited to telephone-grade frequency range. It was never meant for music or video.

Windows can pick either profile as the default when you connect, and it doesn't always pick the right one. Communication apps like Zoom, Teams, and Discord make this worse: when they activate your microphone, Bluetooth is forced to switch from the stereo A2DP profile to the mono Hands-Free profile. That's a hardware-level limitation of Bluetooth — it can't do high-quality stereo and mic at the same time.

Set the correct Bluetooth device as default

The fix is to make sure Windows sends audio to the stereo (Headphones) profile, not the Hands-Free one. Here's how:

  1. Open Sound Settings: press Win+plusR, type ms-settings:sound, press Enter.
  2. Under Output, look at the device dropdown. If it says anything with "Hands-Free" in the name, that's the wrong profile.
  3. Click the dropdown and select the entry that says "Headphones (YourDeviceName)" — the one without "Hands-Free" in its name.
  4. Play some audio. You should hear full stereo sound at normal quality.
This fixes most "connected but no sound" and "robotic audio" problems. If you hear clear stereo audio after switching, you're done. Read on to prevent it from happening again.

What if you need the microphone?

If you need to use your headset's built-in mic for calls, you have two options:

  • Use a separate microphone for calls. Keep the headphones on the stereo profile for output, and use your laptop's built-in mic or a USB microphone for input. This gives you the best of both worlds: high-quality audio and a working mic without the profile switch.
  • Set Hands-Free as the Communications device only. In Sound Settings, scroll down and click "More sound settings" to open the classic Sound control panel. On the Playback tab, right-click the Hands-Free device and select "Set as Default Communication Device." This tells Windows to use it only for calls (Zoom, Teams), while keeping the stereo profile as the default for everything else.
Even with the Communication Device trick, apps that activate the Bluetooth mic will still force a profile switch at the hardware level. The only way to avoid the quality drop entirely during calls is to use a non-Bluetooth microphone.

Bluetooth not connecting at all?

If your Bluetooth device doesn't show up in Sound Settings at all, the problem is at the connection level, not the audio level. Work through this checklist:

  • Is Bluetooth turned on? Press Win+plusR, type ms-settings:bluetooth, press Enter. Make sure the Bluetooth toggle is on.
  • Is your device in pairing mode? Most Bluetooth headphones need to be put into pairing mode manually (usually by holding the power button for 5–7 seconds until an LED flashes). Check your device's manual for the exact method.
  • Is the device paired to another source? Many Bluetooth headphones can only connect to one device at a time. If they're connected to your phone, they won't connect to your PC. Disconnect them from the other device first.
  • Remove and re-pair. In Bluetooth settings (ms-settings:bluetooth), find your device, click the three-dot menu, and select Remove device. Then put your headphones into pairing mode and add them again. This clears out stale pairing data that can cause connection failures.
  • Check the Bluetooth adapter. Open Device Manager (Win+plusX, select Device Manager). Expand Bluetooth. If you see a yellow warning triangle on your Bluetooth adapter, right-click it and select Update driver.
After re-pairing, wait about 10 seconds for Windows to install the audio drivers for the device. You'll see a notification that the device is ready. Only then will it appear in Sound Settings.

Audio cuts out or stutters

If your Bluetooth audio is connected and playing, but it drops out, skips, or stutters, the issue is usually signal quality — not a software setting. Bluetooth audio is surprisingly sensitive to physical conditions.

  • Stay within range. Bluetooth audio quality degrades beyond about 10 meters (33 feet), especially through walls. If you walk to another room, expect dropouts.
  • Reduce wireless interference. Wi-Fi routers, USB 3.0 devices, microwave ovens, and other Bluetooth devices all operate in the 2.4 GHz band and can cause interference. If your PC has a USB 3.0 external drive right next to the Bluetooth adapter, try moving one of them.
  • Check battery level. Low battery on Bluetooth headphones can cause audio artifacts and connection instability. Charge your device and test again.
  • Update your Bluetooth driver. Open Device Manager (Win+plusX, select Device Manager), expand Bluetooth, right-click your adapter, and select Update driver. Alternatively, check your PC manufacturer's support site for a newer Bluetooth driver.
  • Disable Bluetooth power management. Windows may turn off the Bluetooth adapter to save power. In Device Manager, right-click your Bluetooth adapter, select Properties, go to the Power Management tab, and uncheck "Allow the computer to turn off this device to save power."
If stuttering only happens during video calls, it's likely the profile switching problem described above. When your mic activates, Bluetooth switches to the Hands-Free profile, and you may hear a brief audio interruption during the switch.

Prevent Bluetooth from hijacking your default

Every time you connect a Bluetooth audio device, Windows promotes it to the default output. That means if you're happily listening through your desk speakers and then turn on your Bluetooth headphones to charge them (while still near your PC), Windows will silently switch all audio to the headphones. Your desk speakers go silent, and you won't know why until you check Sound Settings.

There are two strategies to prevent this:

1. Disable the Hands-Free profile entirely

If you never use your Bluetooth headset's built-in microphone (because you have a USB mic or use your laptop's mic), you can disable the Hands-Free profile so it never interferes:

  1. Open Sound Settings: Win+plusR, type ms-settings:sound, press Enter.
  2. Scroll down and click "More sound settings" to open the classic Sound control panel.
  3. On the Playback tab, find the Hands-Free entry (it will say "Headset" and include "Hands-Free AG Audio"). Right-click it and select Disable.
  4. Switch to the Recording tab and disable the Hands-Free microphone entry there too.

With the Hands-Free profile disabled, Windows can't accidentally switch to it, and communication apps can't force the profile change. Your headphones will only use the high-quality stereo profile.

2. Disable Bluetooth audio devices when not in use

If you only use Bluetooth headphones occasionally, disable both Bluetooth audio entries (Headphones and Headset) in the Sound control panel when you're done. Windows can't promote a disabled device to default. When you need the headphones again, re-enable the stereo profile.

This is the same principle behind the broader "lock it down" strategy: fewer enabled devices means fewer opportunities for Windows to switch on you. Read Lock it down for the full approach, including how to disable HDMI audio, Stereo Mix, and other phantom devices.
