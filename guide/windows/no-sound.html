No sound on Windows 11? Here's how to fix it.

Windows macOS coming soon Linux coming soon

If your Windows 11 PC suddenly has no sound, the most likely cause is that Windows switched your default audio device. Open Sound Settings (Win+plusR, type ms-settings:sound, Enter), check which output device is selected, and switch it to your actual speakers.

Check your default output device

Windows sends all audio to one device at a time: the default output device. If that device is your monitor's HDMI audio instead of your speakers, you'll hear nothing — even though Windows thinks everything is fine.

Open Sound Settings right now:

Win +plus R type ms-settings:sound Enter

Look at the Output section at the top. The device shown in the dropdown is where Windows is sending your audio right now. If it says something like "LG TV," "Digital Audio (S/PDIF)," or a device you don't recognize — that's your problem.

Click the dropdown and select your actual speakers or headphones.

This single step fixes most "no sound" problems. If you hear audio after switching, you're done. The rest of this page explains why it happened and how to prevent it.

Check volume and mute

If the right device is selected but you still hear nothing, something is muted or turned down. There are more places to check than you'd think.

Is the volume slider at zero? It sounds obvious, but Windows can reset volume levels after updates. Check right now.
  • Windows volume slider — in Sound Settings, make sure the output volume is above zero
  • Windows mute toggle — click the speaker icon next to the volume slider; if there's an XX mark on it, sound is muted
  • Physical device — check that your speakers are powered on and any physical volume knob is turned up
  • App volume — some apps (Zoom, Teams, Spotify, games) have their own volume controls separate from Windows
  • Volume Mixer — press Win+plusR, type sndvol, press Enter to check if any individual app has been muted or turned down independently
The Volume Mixer is the sneakiest one. Windows lets every app have its own volume level, and they persist between sessions. You might have accidentally muted Chrome six months ago and forgotten about it.

Why did your sound stop working?

Your sound was working fine, and then it wasn't. That's not random — something triggered a change. The five most common triggers are:

  1. HDMI cable plugged in or unplugged. Windows sees a monitor as an audio device and may silently switch to it.
  2. Bluetooth device connected or disconnected. Pairing headphones can redirect audio; disconnecting them may not redirect it back.
  3. Windows Update. Updates can reset audio preferences, re-enable disabled devices, or install new audio drivers that change the default.
  4. Driver update. A new audio driver can re-register devices, causing Windows to treat them as "new" and auto-select them.
  5. Apps that change defaults. Zoom, Teams, Discord, and other communication apps can switch your default device when they launch — and not always switch it back when they close.

If you want the full technical explanation of how and why Windows makes these decisions, read Why it happens.

Still no sound? Test every device

If you've checked the default and the volume and still hear nothing, the next step is to systematically test each audio device. Select each output device in Sound Settings one at a time and play audio after each switch. This tells you whether the problem is a wrong default or a deeper issue with a specific device.

We have a complete walkthrough for this: Test your devices. It covers how to cycle through every output and input device, what to listen for, and how to identify which device actually works.

If none of your devices produce sound, the issue may be a driver problem or a hardware failure. The testing guide will help you narrow that down.

Prevent it from happening again

Once you've fixed the immediate problem, the question is: how do you stop Windows from switching devices on you again?

The answer is straightforward: disable every audio device you don't actually use. Windows can only switch to a device that's enabled. If your monitor's HDMI audio, Stereo Mix, and that mystery USB audio device are all disabled, Windows has nowhere to wander off to.
