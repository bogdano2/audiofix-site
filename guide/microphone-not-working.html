Microphone not working on Windows 11?

If your microphone stopped picking up your voice, Windows probably switched your default input device. Open Sound Settings (Win+plusR, type ms-settings:sound, Enter), scroll to Input, and check which device is selected. If it says Stereo Mix or an unfamiliar name, that's your problem — switch it to your real microphone.

Check your default input device

Windows records audio from one device at a time: the default input device. If that device is set to Stereo Mix (a virtual loopback that captures system audio instead of your voice), a disconnected Bluetooth headset, or a line-in port with nothing plugged into it, your mic will appear to be "not working" — even though the hardware is fine.

Open Sound Settings right now:

Win +plus R type ms-settings:sound Enter

Scroll down to the Input section. The device shown in the dropdown is where Windows is listening for your voice right now.

Sound Settings — Input
  • Microphone (Realtek Audio) YOUR MIC
  • Stereo Mix (Realtek Audio) IMPOSTOR
  • Headset (WH-1000XM5 Hands-Free)

Click the dropdown and select your actual microphone. If you're not sure which one it is, look for a name that includes "Microphone" plus your audio hardware brand (Realtek, USB, your headset model).

This single step fixes most "mic not working" problems. Speak into the mic — if the input level meter starts moving, you're done. The rest of this page covers the other causes.

Check microphone privacy settings

Windows 11 has a privacy toggle that controls whether apps are allowed to access your microphone at all. If this toggle is off, every app will behave as if you have no microphone — Zoom will say "no mic detected," Discord will show silence, and the input level meter in Sound Settings won't move.

Open the microphone privacy page directly:

Win +plus R type ms-settings:privacy-microphone Enter

Check these two toggles:

  • Microphone access — the master switch at the top. This must be On.
  • Let apps access your microphone — the second toggle. This must also be On.

Below those toggles, you'll see a list of individual apps with their own mic permission toggles. If a specific app can't hear you, check that its toggle is on in this list.

If the master Microphone access toggle is off, no app can use your mic. This is the single most common cause of "mic not working" when the correct device is already selected. Windows Updates have been known to reset this toggle.
Desktop apps vs. Store apps: Traditional desktop apps (like the desktop version of Zoom or Discord) are controlled by the "Let desktop apps access your microphone" toggle at the bottom of the page. If you only turned on permissions for Store apps, your desktop apps may still be blocked.

Test the mic level meter

The fastest way to confirm your microphone is working is to watch the input level meter in Sound Settings. It shows real-time audio input — if the bar moves when you speak or clap, Windows is receiving audio from your mic.

  1. Open Sound Settings (Win+plusR, type ms-settings:sound, Enter).
  2. Scroll to Input and make sure your microphone is selected.
  3. Speak into the mic at normal volume, or clap near it.
  4. Watch the input level bar next to your device — it should bounce in response.
What the mic level meter result means and what to do next
Result What it means
Bar moves when you speak Your mic is working at the OS level. If a specific app still can't hear you, the problem is in that app's settings.
Bar doesn't move at all Windows is not receiving audio. Check: correct device selected, privacy permissions on, mic volume above zero, no physical mute engaged.
Bar is maxed out / clipping constantly Mic gain is too high. Click your input device, then lower the Input volume slider.

For a more thorough test — including how to test each input device one by one — see the full walkthrough at Test your devices.

Check for hardware mute

Before you go deeper into software settings, check the microphone itself. Many headsets and microphones have physical mute controls that are easy to trigger accidentally — and they override everything Windows does.

Common hardware mute mechanisms that catch people off guard:
  • Boom arm flip-to-mute: Many gaming headsets (HyperX, SteelSeries, Corsair) mute the mic when you flip the boom arm up. You won't see any indicator in Windows.
  • Inline mute button: Some headsets have a mute switch on the cable itself, partway between the headset and the plug.
  • USB mic mute button: Microphones like the Blue Yeti and Elgato Wave have a capacitive or physical mute button on the body. A red light usually indicates muted.
  • Keyboard mute key: Many laptops and keyboards have a dedicated mic mute key (often F4 or a function key with a mic icon). Check if there's a muted-mic indicator on your screen.

If you find a hardware mute engaged, toggle it off and re-check the input level meter in Sound Settings. The bar should start moving when you speak.

Why does the mic work in some apps but not others?

If your microphone works in Sound Settings (the level meter moves) but a specific app can't hear you, the problem is almost certainly the app's own audio settings — not Windows.

Apps like Zoom, Microsoft Teams, Discord, and OBS each have their own input device selector that overrides the Windows default. If Zoom is set to use "Headset (Bluetooth)" but you're using a USB mic, Zoom won't hear you — even though Windows is picking up your voice just fine.

Where to check:

  • Zoom: Settings Audio Microphone dropdown
  • Teams: Settings Devices Microphone dropdown
  • Discord: User Settings Voice & Video Input Device
The Communications device complication: Windows actually has two separate default input devices — the standard Default and a special Communications device. Some voice apps use the Communications role instead of the standard Default. You can check both in the old Sound control panel: press Win+plusR, type mmsys.cpl, Enter, then click the Recording tab.

For the full explanation of why Windows has two default devices and how apps choose between them, see Why it happens.

Prevent it from happening again

Now that your microphone is working, the question is: how do you stop Windows from switching away from it next time?

The answer is the same as for output devices: disable every input device you don't actually use. Windows can only switch your default to a device that's enabled. If Stereo Mix, unused line-in ports, and old Bluetooth headset profiles are all disabled, Windows has nowhere to drift to.

The most common impostor to disable is Stereo Mix. Stereo Mix is a virtual device that captures your computer's output audio (what you hear through your speakers) and presents it as microphone input. It's useful for recording system audio, but if Windows accidentally selects it as your default input, every app will "hear" your computer's audio playback instead of your voice — or hear nothing at all if no audio is playing.

To disable Stereo Mix quickly:

  1. Press Win+plusR, type mmsys.cpl, press Enter.
  2. Click the Recording tab.
  3. Right-click Stereo Mix and select Disable.
  4. While you're here, right-click and disable any other input devices you never use.

For the full walkthrough on identifying and disabling all unused audio devices (both input and output), see Lock it down.
