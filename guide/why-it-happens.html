Why your audio keeps breaking

This page applies to all operating systems.

It's not you. It's too many variables between your operating system and your ears—and any one of them can silently break the chain. The concepts on this page apply to Windows, macOS, and Linux alike.

Audio is a chain, and every link can break

Your sound travels through a chain: App, OS default, driver (the software that connects the OS to the hardware), physical device, your ears. For microphones, it's reversed. A break at any point means silence—or the wrong device.

App OS Default Driver Device You

Your OS has too many devices

Windows registers every audio-capable device it has ever seen—HDMI monitors, docking stations, Bluetooth headsets, USB webcams, virtual drivers. A typical PC has 4–8 output devices and 3–5 input devices. Most have useless names.

▸ [1] Speakers (Realtek(R) Audio)            DEFAULT
  [2] LG TV (Intel(R) Display Audio)
  [3] Digital Audio (S/PDIF)
  [4] Speakers (USB PnP Audio Device)       ← what is this?
  [5] Headphones (Realtek(R) Audio)

  • Realtek(R) Audio—your motherboard or laptop's built-in audio chip. Usually your main speakers or headphone jack.
  • Intel(R) Display Audio—HDMI or DisplayPort audio routed through your GPU to a monitor or TV.
  • Digital Audio (S/PDIF)—an optical or coaxial output, often unused on modern PCs.
  • USB PnP Audio Device—a catch-all name for docking stations, USB webcams, USB hubs with audio, or cheap USB sound adapters.
  • Headphones (Realtek)—the same Realtek chip, but detected as headphones when something is plugged into the 3.5mm jack.
  • Stereo Mix—a virtual input device that captures whatever your speakers are playing. The audio equivalent of that junk drawer everyone has but nobody opens.

Five things that silently move your default

You plug in your monitor and suddenly your music is playing through its tinny built-in speakers. The "default device" is just a label your OS sticks on one device at a time—and these events can move that label without asking you:

  1. Plugging in HDMI / DisplayPort
    Your GPU registers a new audio endpoint for the display, and the OS promotes recently-activated devices to default.
  2. Bluetooth connect / disconnect
    Each reconnection registers as a new device arrival, because the OS treats every Bluetooth connect event as a fresh plug-in.
  3. Windows Update
    Updates can replace audio drivers, which re-registers endpoints from scratch—resetting your preferences in the process.
  4. Driver installation
    New drivers create new Plug and Play device nodes, which the OS sees as brand-new devices and may promote to default.
  5. Apps with their own audio settings
    Zoom, Teams, Discord, and others can switch to their own preferred device, ignoring the OS default entirely.

Windows uses a Plug and Play (PnP) event system. Every time a device connects or disconnects, the OS re-evaluates the device list. The audio subsystem (Core Audio, via IMMDeviceEnumerator) maintains a priority ranking for endpoints.

When a new device appears, Windows often promotes it to default using a "most recently activated" heuristic. There is no built-in way to say "never change my default"—which is exactly why the problem keeps coming back.

This isn't new. It's been this way for 30 years.

Windows Vista rebuilt the audio API from the ground up (WASAPI replaced the old waveOut/DirectSound stack), but the underlying Plug and Play device-arrival behavior—and the lack of a "pin my default and never change it" mechanism—has persisted since Windows 95. The same PnP architecture that made sense for one sound card and one set of speakers now manages a dozen endpoints, and no OS has added a reliable way to lock it down.

Output breaks and input breaks look different

You join a video call and nobody can hear you, even though you can hear them perfectly. That asymmetry is the clue: output and input are separate defaults, and they break in different ways.

Output (speakers / headphones)

  • No sound at all
  • Sound from wrong speakers (monitor, turned-off display)
  • Sound was working, then stopped after plugging something in

Input (microphone)

  • Nobody can hear you on calls
  • Wrong mic active (webcam across room instead of USB mic)
  • "Stereo Mix" capturing desktop audio instead of your voice

Volume and mute—the obvious check nobody does

Before anything else: Is the volume at zero? Is the device muted (hardware or software)? Is the physical device actually turned on? These account for a surprising number of "broken audio" reports.
